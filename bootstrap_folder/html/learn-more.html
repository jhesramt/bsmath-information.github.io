What is Bachelor of Science in Mathematics?

Curriculum

Within a Bachelor of Science in Mathematics program, students are expected to fulfill a total of 120 credit hours. About half of these credits are dedicated to general education requirements, while the other fifty percent is allocated to mathematics courses. Around 30 credits are focused on establishing the core foundations of the major, and an additional 30 credits are designated for upper-level math electives. This degree serves as a solid foundation for those pursuing advanced studies in mathematics, including master's and doctoral degrees in mathematics or applied mathematics.

Lower-Division Courses

During the first two years in the program, students typically take a number of general education requirements and calculus. General education courses give students a broad liberal arts education, exposing them to many different cultures and ideas. Examples of possible general education courses are Gender Society, NSTP, Purposive Communication. Another major requirement for any Bachelor of Science program in mathematics is a three-semester course in calculus. This course is the study of rates of changes of functions, and covers topics such as derivatives and integrals, both in single and multiple variables

Foundational Courses

In addition to calculus 1, 2 and 3, students pursuing Bachelor of Science degrees in math also have to take a number of other foundational courses. Requirements vary, but most programs require students to take courses such as linear algebra, abstract algebra, logic and differential equations. Occasionally, programs also require students to take a course in analysis, also known as real analysis or modern analysis. These courses introduce students to the three primary tracks that all advanced mathematics separate into: algebra, analysis and applied mathematics.

Electives for Pure Mathematics
  • Number Theory: Explores properties of integers and their relationships.
  • Abstract Algebra: Focuses on algebraic structures like groups, rings, and fields.
  • Topology: Studies properties of space that are preserved under continuous transformations.
  • Real Analysis: Examines properties of real numbers and functions of a real variable.
  • Complex Analysis: Deals with functions of complex numbers and their properties.
Electives for Statistics
  • Regression Analysis: Involves modeling relationships between variables, a crucial technique in statistical modeling.
  • Statistical Computing: Introduces computational methods used in statistical analysis, integrating computation with statistical techniques.
  • Categorical Data Analysis: Studies properties of space that are preserved under continuous transformations.
  • Non Parametric: Understand and apply these methods in analyzing data without strict assumptions about the underlying distribution
Electives for CIT
  • Web Development: Introduces concepts and technologies for creating web applications..
  • Multimedia: Explores principles of design applied to digital media, including graphic design, typography, and layout for multimedia projects..
  • Python: Focused on data analysis covers NumPy and Pandas for numerical computing and data manipulation, integrates data visualization through Matplotlib other libraries to create visual representations, and employs Python for statistical analysis, data cleaning, and transformation processes
  • Android Studio: Integrated development environment (IDE) for building Android applications.

Career and Job Opportunities

Graduates of BS Mathematics often obtain jobs in education (teaching in high school math courses or tertiary level elementary/service courses), statistics, actuarial science, operations research, risk management, business and economics, banking and finance, and computing and information technology

BS Math program often covers fundamental mathematical and statistical concepts, making it a strong foundation for pursuing roles in statistics.

Bachelor of Science in Mathematics (BS Math) provides an excellent foundation for becoming an actuary. Actuaries require strong mathematical, statistical, and analytical skills, which are cultivated through a math program. The curriculum often includes courses in probability, statistics, financial mathematics, and risk management, all of which are directly relevant to actuarial work
